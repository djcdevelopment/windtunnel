Measured results — experiments complete (2026-07-10)

Experiments 1 and 2 (the two [IDLE-JUDGE] runs) finished on idle OMEN/AM4. Final numbers are folded into R1/R2 below; three headline signals:

  • The judge is near-deterministic on repeat; the noise is which lens/model you pick. Repeat-scoring the same judge/rubric (K=6, full 192 cells) gives a within-cell sampling std of just 0.40 pt (median 0.00) — over half the cells scored identically all six times — against ~4.6–6.5 pt disagreement across rubrics/models. Repeat votes buy almost nothing; the gate's leverage is diversity. Resolving the measured +3.05-pt concise-author effect takes ~1 repeat vote but ~19 lens-diverse votes.

  • The third judge model failed — and it's the lab's own hardware finding. AM4 oxen-planner failed most of its scores; root cause is the 32 GB DDR4 host-RAM ceiling (AM4's preflight refuses to start the planner → 502), not a token-budget or format fault — and it's miscalibrated besides (scores 1–98). Group A — the judge (how to build the gate)

R1 · Size the quality gate to many lens-diverse votes — a single lens is unsafe. STRONG

Backed by On our 192 re-judged finals, one rubric lens flips the sign of a real effect (concise-author reads +2.8 under the neutral judge but −0.2 under completeness), and single-lens estimates inflate up to 2.86×. New, sharper number: the per-cell disagreement across the three rubrics averages 6.5 pts (median 7, max 47) — that is the noise a gate faces at the level it actually decides, per item. A 1-vote gate has a one-in-three chance of reverting a genuine gain.

Measured (final) The full run (192 cells, K=6) closes it: within-judge sampling std is just 0.40 pt (median 0.00) — the OMEN judge is near-deterministic on repeat, so repeat votes add almost nothing. All the real uncertainty is lens/model choice (~4.6–6.5 pt). Reframed rule, now with a number: don't spend votes on repeats — spend them on diverse lenses/models. To resolve the measured +3.05-pt concise-author effect at 2σ takes ~1 repeat vote but ~19 lens-diverse votes.

Method (done) [IDLE-JUDGE] re-scored all 192 finals K=6× under the neutral rubric on OMEN and decomposed within-cell (sampling) vs between-lens variance. Zero regeneration; harness looped score_proposal. ~1 night on idle OMEN.

Decided the real --gate-votes is ~19 lens-diverse votes (≈1 if you only repeat one judge); the dominant noise is lens/model choice, not sampling — so the gate budget should buy diverse judges, not repeats.

R2 · Diversify judge models, not just rubrics — on reliable, in-format judges. DIRECTIONAL

Backed by Every prior "multi-lens" result varied the rubric on a single model (OMEN qwen3-coder:30b). The scorer already supports a multi-model panel.

Measured (final) A 2nd judge model (OMEN qwen2.5:14b), full 192 finals: cross-model per-cell |diff| is 4.6 pt (median 7, max 55) — comparable to, slightly below the ~6.5-pt cross-rubric spread, not the larger figure I'd guessed. So for same-family Qwen judges, model diversity ≈ rubric diversity — both matter, neither dominates — and both models rank concise-author top (qwen3-coder 91.2, qwen2.5 87.2). The intended 3rd model, AM4 oxen-planner, is disqualified: it kept crashing the card-0 endpoint under load — root cause is AM4's 32 GB DDR4 host-RAM ceiling (preflight refusal → 502), the lab's own hardware finding, not a token-budget/format fault — and its scores are miscalibrated (1–98). Full debugging log: OxenTODO. Two lessons: a real model-diversity gate needs per-node liveness/retry, and the terse 300-token "SCORE: N" format is worth revisiting — a reasoning judge with a real budget may calibrate better.

Still open whether a genuinely different model family (not two Qwens) decorrelates more than rubrics do — the current pair is same-family, which likely understates the model axis.

Decides whether "diversify judge lenses" should mean different models, different rubrics, or both — and which models can even judge in-format.

Group B — the mechanisms (what to change in the loop)

R3 · Adopt the concise-author prompt — it's the highest-scoring and most judge-robust arm. DIRECTIONAL

Backed by +2.8 vs baseline under the neutral judge, flat-high ~91 across laps 1–6, wins under every rubric. And the new cut: concise-author has the lowest cross-rubric per-cell disagreement of any arm (2.6 pts) vs baseline 9.0 and thorough-critic 10.8 — different judge philosophies agree on it. It doesn't just score well, it scores stably. A mechanism autoreason's library doesn't have yet.

Still unknown whether it transfers to David's tasks/models, and whether it survives a pairwise tournament judge (ours is absolute — see R6).

Experiment [IDLE-JUDGE] after R6, pairwise re-score our existing finals (concise vs baseline) to check the effect survives the scoring-mode switch; then [IDLE-GEN] run concise-author as an arm inside the autoreason inner loop on his tasks.

Decides whether concise-author is a portable, drop-in inner-loop mechanism or a Qwen-plus-absolute-judge artifact.

R4 · Cap laps rather than refine open-ended — it's nearly free. STRONG

Backed by under a fair (neutral) judge, scores are flat ~87–92 across laps 1–4; capping L4→L2 erases the baseline's −10.7 default-judge collapse at ≈0 neutral-judge cost. The lap cap, not convergence, was the real control knob in every round (critic converged on 0/12 cells).

Still unknown only tested to lap 6; and because our critic never converged, "cap" and "converge" were never cleanly separated.

Experiment [IDLE-GEN] one extended run to laps 8–10 to confirm no late cliff. Low priority — the L1–6 flatness already largely settles it.

Decides the safe default lap cap for both the commander lane and his inner loop.

R5 · Treat "escalate critic difficulty" as a tax, not a default fix. DIRECTIONAL

Backed by our thorough-critic arm is that mechanism (demand exhaustive coverage) and it was the worst arm (80.2 default, −0.1 vs baseline even on the neutral judge), degrading at every lap. Mechanistic new cut: thorough-critic also has the highest cross-rubric disagreement (10.8 pts) — escalation produces verbose outputs whose quality is judge-dependent. It doesn't just score lower, it makes the score less trustworthy.

Still unknown autoreason applies this mechanism for premature convergence — a signature our loop never hit (we had the opposite failure). So we've measured its cost, not its benefit where it's meant to fire.

Experiment [IDLE-GEN] induce premature convergence (lower the convergence bar in refine.py so cells stop early), then A/B critic-escalation on/off, judged under R1's multi-vote gate. Measure net effect in-regime.

Decides whether his premature-convergence fix is net-positive where it triggers, or whether raising the convergence bar directly dominates it at lower quality cost.

Group C — the enabling build

R6 · Build a pairwise-judge mode. NEEDS BUILD

Backed by our judge scores each response absolutely (0–100, one at a time); autoreason's tournament is pairwise (A/B/AB). Two consequences: our mechanism results aren't yet proven under his scoring regime, and his position_bias signature cannot exist in our data — there's no A/B position to bias.

Still unknown whether the concise-author / lap-cap effects survive pairwise scoring, and how large position bias is on a Qwen judge.

Experiment [BUILD] add pairwise_score(A, B) that presents both orders and records the swap; position_bias = order-swap disagreement rate. ~half a day; then it feeds R3 and gives R2's panel a pairwise variant.

Decides unlocks the transfer test (R3) and the only clean measure of his position_bias — the prerequisite that turns "run it in his loop" from aspiration into a wired path.
