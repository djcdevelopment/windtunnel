Matrix Wind-Tunnel — Lab Log

Living document · started 2026-07-05 · the mechnet's planning-quality experiment: does self-refinement actually improve planning, and does it depend on the model / hardware / task? Two-economies doctrine — laps on electricity across AM4 (2× Intel B70) + OMEN (RTX 5070). Updated as runs land.

Bottom line (read this first)

Six rounds of idle-hardware experiments on refinement laps for planning, ending with a confound check that rewrote the conclusion:

  • Lap count barely matters under fair (length-neutral) judging — quality sits ~87–92 across 1–4 laps. The early "laps hurt → 2 laps best → over-refinement collapse" arc was largely an artifact of the default judge's brevity bias (it penalized the verbosity that laps accumulate).
  • The one robust, real effect: a concise author system prompt ("shortest complete answer; lead with the decision") — a small (~+3) but consistent edge that survives every judge and stays flat across laps. That's the actionable change for the commander lane.
  • Biggest lesson — the judge is a first-order confound. A single LLM judge's rubric shaped the results more than the thing under test. This applies directly to the lab's belief/assay layer: LLM-scored agent work needs multi-rubric / multi-judge scoring, or the numbers measure the ruler.

The value wasn't the answer about laps (it's "meh") — it was the method: the wind tunnel flagged an effect, corrected it, explained it, then caught its own measurement bias. All on idle local electricity, overnight.

What we built (the instrument)

Commander intent lane LIVE

A local author↔critic refine loop that takes frontier out of the run loop (ADR-0012). hearth/commander/ + door tools refine_idea/refine_result + CLI. This loop is the cell engine of the matrix.

Matrix dataset harness LIVE

Finding 3 — the critic never converged pilot

converged=False on all 12 cells. The critic never emitted VERDICT: CONVERGED — it always found something to revise, so every cell burned its full lap budget. "Refine until converged" would run forever here; the lap cap is what stops it. The convergence bar (or critic prompt) needs a look — and it means the lap count, not convergence, is the real control knob.

Hardware finding — 32GB DDR4 is the binding constraint, not VRAM confirmed

The dedicated-loop run surfaced an infra truth flagged from the very start: the B70s hold 64GB VRAM total, but AM4 has only 32GB DDR4 host RAM. SYCL llama-server keeps host-side KV + compute buffers even with -ngl 99; co-loading the 30B planner + 14B critic at generous contexts OOM-killed the planner mid-run (503s until systemd restarted it). Dropping the critic 32k→8k ctx freed ~7 GiB (used 23→16 GiB) and restored headroom. The old Windows vllama had a max_host_used_gb_preflight: 22.0 gate for exactly this — the Linux serving has none yet (a real follow-up). Runbook: B70-CARD-MANAGEMENT.md.

Round 5 — the over-refinement collapse is a prompt artifact, not a law AUTHORITATIVE · 192/192, n=12/arm·lap

Round 4's inverted-U raised the real question: is the L3–L4 collapse inherent to refining, or is the critic pushing bloat the judge then penalizes? So we swept 4 prompt-variant arms across the full grid (memory-safe cross-machine). The answer is decisive — the collapse is caused by the prompt, and a better prompt removes it entirely.

Arm (what it changes)L1L2L3L4overall
baseline85.284.284.374.582.1
minimalist-critic (reward brevity)88.886.886.286.587.1
thorough-critic (demand coverage)84.580.379.276.880.2
concise-author (shortest complete)91.490.888.090.290.1
  • The collapse is prompt-driven. baseline dives at L4 (→74.5); minimalist-critic stays flat (~86 across all laps — no collapse). The critic pushing complexity was the cause.
  • The author prompt dominates the critic prompt. concise-author is the best arm AND immune to over-refinement (90.2 at L4). "Write the shortest complete answer" beats every critic tweak — a bigger, simpler lever.
  • thorough-critic is actively harmful (80.2, worst) — demanding exhaustiveness degrades planning at every lap. Mechanism confirmed.

Follow-up confirms it (72/72, laps 1–6): the stacked winners (concise author + minimalist critic) hold flat-high (~90) across all six laps — 87.3 / 91.3 / 91.3 / 85.7 / 90.8 / 88.5 (overall 89.2) — while baseline degrades and gets erratic (88→77.8→82.8→72.3→76.2, overall 80.9). Prompt design fully defeats the over-refinement collapse: with the right prompt, lap count stops mattering.

One loophole left, tested next: is "concise wins" partly the single OMEN judge rewarding directness? A re-judge of the same outputs under a completeness-favoring rubric settles whether concise answers are genuinely better planning or just judge-preferred.

Round 6 — the judge was a confound; the effect is real but smaller honest caveat · 192 finals re-judged

Before trusting any of the above: the score came from ONE judge (OMEN qwen3-coder) whose rubric included "directness". Does that reward brevity and inflate concise-author? We re-judged all 192 finals under two alternative rubrics — no regeneration, same outputs — and it changed the story.

Armdefault (directness)completeness judgeneutral judge
baseline82.190.488.1
concise-author90.190.290.9
minimalist-critic87.189.189.8
thorough-critic80.290.388.0
  • The finding survives — concise-author still wins under the neutral judge (90.9, top of the ranking). It's genuinely better planning, not just short answers the judge liked.
  • But the effect was inflated ~3x. The default judge's brevity bias stretched concise's lead to +8; length-neutral, it's +2.8 over baseline.
  • The completeness judge collapses the spread (all arms ~90): baseline and thorough-critic jump from ~81 → ~90. So much of the "over-refinement collapse" and arm spread was the default judge penalizing verbosity, not real quality loss.

Definitive — the collapse was largely a judge artifact. Re-judging per lap under the neutral rubric: baseline goes 90.2 / 87.4 / 89.1 / 86.5 (a mild −3.7 drift, NO cliff) where the default judge showed a −10.7 collapse to 74.5. concise-author is dead flat and top at every lap (91.4 / 91.4 / 91.1 / 91.7). So under fair measurement, lap count barely matters (everything sits ~87–92 across 1–4 laps); the one robust effect is the concise author prompt's small, consistent edge.

Meta-lesson (the biggest one): the judge rubric is a first-order confound — the Round 4–5 "inverted-U / collapse" was mostly the instrument. Always test the judge. This lands on the lab's own belief/assay layer: LLM-scored agent work needs multi-rubric judging, or the scores measure the ruler as much as the work.

What this changes calibrated by Round 6

  • A concise author prompt is a real, modest win. Setting the commander lane's author system prompt to concise ("shortest complete, buildable answer; lead with the decision; no preamble") helps ~+3 under a fair judge (not the +8 the default judge suggested). Worth doing; not a silver bullet.
  • Lap count matters less than it first looked — much of the "over-refinement collapse" was the default judge penalizing the verbosity that more laps produce. Don't hard-cap laps on that basis; a concise author + a fair judge is the better fix. (Pending the per-lap re-judge to quantify exactly.)
  • The biggest takeaway is about measurement, not laps: the judge rubric was a first-order confound that inflated every effect ~3x. This lands directly on the lab's own belief/assay layer — it scores agent work with LLM judges too. Judge-robustness (multi-rubric / multi-judge) belongs in the assay, or the scores measure the ruler as much as the work.
  • The matrix earned its keep four times: found an effect → corrected it → explained it → then caught its own measurement bias. Idle local hardware, self-funding — and self-skeptical — knowledge. That last step is the one most experiments skip.
