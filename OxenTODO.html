OxenTODO — the oxen-planner judge, a debugging log

2026-07-10 · what happened when we tried to add AM4's oxen-planner (Qwen3-30B-A3B on a B70 card) as a third judge model, how it failed, the wrong turn we took, how we caught it, and what we did about it. Written as a working log — the open items are at the bottom. Context: RECOMMENDATIONS.html R2, crossover map.

TL;DR

We tried three judge models for the "diversify the judge" experiment. Two (OMEN qwen3-coder:30b, qwen2.5:14b) worked cleanly. The third — AM4 oxen-planner — failed most of its scores. We first blamed the 300-token judge budget; a direct test refuted that (it scores fine at 300). The real cause, from the AM4 journal, is the lab's own documented hardware finding: the 32 GB DDR4 host-RAM ceiling. AM4's preflight guard refuses to start the planner when host RAM is already near the limit, so the judge endpoint 502s. Net: oxen-planner is not a usable judge here (unreliable under load + miscalibrated scores), the reliable cross-model result is the two OMEN models, and the open items are listed below.

1 · What we tried

Recommendation R2 says: to trust a self-improving loop's quality gate, diversify the judge by model, not just by rubric. To measure that, re-score the 192 planning finals under the neutral rubric with a panel of three distinct judge models:

judge modelhostpathoutcome
qwen3-coder:30bOMEN (RTX 5070)OllamaCLEAN 192/192
qwen2.5:14bOMEN (RTX 5070)OllamaCLEAN 192/192
oxen-planner (Qwen3-30B-A3B)AM4 (2× Intel B70)oxen facade :8090 → llama-server :8080FAILED most cells

The AM4 leg was the interesting one — a different serving stack (SYCL llama-server on Intel B70) and a genuinely different deployment, exactly the kind of judge diversity R2 is about.

2 · How it broke

The first panel sweep recorded 192 oxen-planner rows, but 167 were None — only 25 valid scores, and those only on two of the four arms:

am4-oxen-planner   total=192  valid= 25  none=167  arms={baseline:23, minimalist-critic:2}
omen-qwen3-coder   total=192  valid=192  none=0
omen-qwen2.5:14b   total=192  valid=192  none=0

The sweep script stored no error text on the failed rows — just score: null — so at first the failure was shapeless. That missing error field is the first lesson (see TODO).

3 · The wrong turn — and how we caught it

HYPOTHESIS (WRONG) oxen-planner is a reasoning-heavy model; it probably emitted a long chain-of-thought and overran the 300-token judge budget before printing SCORE:. Plausible, and it got written into an early draft of R2.

VERIFICATION Rather than ship the guess, we ran the judge prompt directly at two budgets:

oxen-planner  max_tokens=300   ok=True  chars=9   -> "SCORE: 95"
oxen-planner  max_tokens=2048  ok=True  chars=9   -> "SCORE: 94"

Refuted. oxen-planner answers in ~9 characters — it does not overrun 300 tokens. The budget was never the problem. (The valid residue of the idea — that a terse bare-score judge might be worth replacing with a reasoning judge — survives as a separate open thread; it just wasn't this bug.)

THE TELL With the format theory dead, we looked at the failure pattern in file order — and it wasn't random:

score present(.) / None(X), in order:
....X.....................XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX…(X to the end)

The first ~25 finals scored clean, then every remaining cell failed. That "clean, then dead to the end" shape is a died-partway signature — an availability failure, not a model/format one. The endpoint went down mid-sweep and never recovered, and with no retry the script silently logged 167 nulls.

4 · The real cause — the lab's own hardware finding

A retry-hardened re-run (below) eventually surfaced the concrete error the first sweep had swallowed — HTTP 502 Bad Gateway from the facade, meaning the facade is up but its upstream (the card-0 llama-server on :8080) is down. The AM4 journal says exactly why:

b70-planner.service: Started - B70 planner - Qwen3-30B single-card SYCL0 :8080
serve-planner.sh: b70-preflight REFUSED: planner — host RAM already 28GiB used
  (> 27GiB ceiling, 32GB DDR4). Not starting to avoid an OOM cascade.
  Free a slot ('systemctl --user stop b70-critic') or lower a context.
b70-planner.service: Failed with result 'exit-code'   (looping every ~5s)

This is the wind-tunnel's own documented constraint — "32 GB DDR4 is the binding constraint, not VRAM." The B70s hold 64 GB of VRAM, but the AM4 host has only 32 GB of DDR4, and llama-server keeps host-side KV/compute buffers. Under sustained judge load the host RAM sits near the ceiling; AM4's own preflight guard then correctly refuses to (re)start the planner to avoid an OOM cascade. So the judge endpoint stays down → 502 → null scores.

The evaluation-layer failure and the hardware finding are the same finding. It isn't the model, the prompt, or the token budget — it's the 32 GB ceiling, exactly where the lab said the pressure would be.

Second strike: the scores are miscalibrated too

Even on the cells oxen-planner did score, the values run 1–98 with genuine 1 outliers on ordinary planning responses — a scale it isn't holding to (0–100). So beyond the reliability problem, it's a noisy judge. Two independent reasons it's unfit for this panel.

5 · What we did about it

  • Retry-hardened re-run (hearth/experiments/rejudge_oxen.py): per-cell retry ×4 with linear backoff, a 30 s cooldown after consecutive failures (let an evicted model reload), and — the fix for lesson #1 — an error field on every row. This salvaged 100 valid scores across all four arms (baseline 40, minimalist 23, thorough 23, concise 14) vs the first sweep's 25 on two arms, before the host-RAM ceiling took the endpoint down hard.
  • Banked the reliable result. The two OMEN models give a clean, full-192 cross-model number (per-cell |diff| ~4.6, comparable to the ~6.5 cross-rubric spread). That is the R2 answer; oxen-planner is documented as unsuitable, not silently dropped.
  • Corrected the record openly. The wrong token-budget claim is fixed in R2 and logged in the RECOMMENDATIONS corrections log — the mistake stays visible on purpose.
  • Did not bulldoze the machine. The fix (systemctl --user stop b70-critic to free a slot) is a change to AM4's running config, left for the operator to decide rather than forced mid-run. A wake_am4 attempt was correctly refused by the same preflight guard — the guard is working as designed.
